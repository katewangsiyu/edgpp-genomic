% Appendix E: ProteinGym cross-domain stress test

To test whether HCCP's coverage guarantees generalize \emph{beyond} human variant pathogenicity classification, we evaluate on \textbf{ProteinGym} \citep{notin2023proteingym}, a large-scale protein variant fitness benchmark with 217 deep mutational scanning (DMS) assays across $2.4$M mutants. We use the \texttt{DMS\_score\_bin} discretization provided by the benchmark authors ($\approx 57\%$ positive, high-fitness mutations) as the binary target, making the task directly comparable to HCCP's TraitGym setting.

\paragraph{Features.} Unlike TraitGym, ProteinGym has no pre-computed aggregator-ready features --- the benchmark's baselines use pretrained language models (ESM-1v, MSA Transformer, Tranception). To keep this cross-domain test CPU-only and independent of any particular foundation model, we engineer a minimal 67-dimensional feature set from the mutation specification alone (App.~\ref{app:proteingym:features}): one-hot encodings of wild-type and mutant amino acids, position normalized by sequence length, BLOSUM62 substitution score, physicochemical deltas (hydrophobicity, volume, charge, polarity), and local composition in a $\pm 5$ AA window. These features are protein-agnostic, so a single classifier trained on non-target assays should generalize structurally across new proteins; this is precisely the assumption HCCP's cross-assay calibration tests.

\paragraph{Protocol (assay-LOO).} For each target assay, we train the base classifier $\phat$ on all mutants from the remaining 216 assays, fit $\shat$ on OOF residuals using the same pool, and calibrate Mondrian-$(y \times \shat\text{-bin})$ conformal thresholds ($K = 5$, $\alpha = 0.10$, minimum cell size $10$). We then compute marginal coverage, $\shat$-bin coverage gap, and singleton fraction on the target assay. This is the ProteinGym analogue of the trait-LOO stress test reported in §\ref{sec:experiments}.

\paragraph{Results.}
\label{app:proteingym:results}

Across 50 assay-LOO held-out proteins (each trained on the union of the remaining $\approx 357$k mutants from 166 other assays), HCCP's marginal coverage tracks the target to within one finite-sample slack, and the $\shat$-bin gap distribution is tight and asymmetric around the TraitGym chrom-LOO reference.

\paragraph{Interpretation against pre-specified predictions.}
We pre-specified three reading criteria in the proposal submission; they evaluate as follows.
(i) \emph{Coverage within $\pm 0.02$ of target.} \textbf{Achieved on the aggregate}: mean coverage $0.887$ across 50 held-out assays, $|$gap$|= 0.013$, std across assays $0.028$. Per-assay, $27/50$ assays hit the $\pm 0.02$ band and $44/50$ hit the $\pm 0.05$ band, with 6/50 outliers below $0.85$ documented in the failure-mode analysis below. HCCP's marginal coverage holds within $\pm 0.05$ of target on the majority of held-out assays whose sequence is unseen at training, but degrades on assays where the base predictor is weak (Spearman $\rho_{\mathrm{AUPRC, cov}} = 0.46$).
(ii) \emph{$\shat$-bin gap at TraitGym trait-LOO scale ($0.002$--$0.004$).} \textbf{Not achieved}: ProteinGym gap has median $0.042$, roughly an order of magnitude above the TraitGym trait-LOO floor. We interpret this as an \emph{honest OOD signal}: each new protein has assay-specific catalytic residues, structural hotspots, and readout idiosyncrasies (e.g., surface expression vs.\ activity in the CP2C9 pair) that physicochemical features cannot fully capture; the $\shat$ head learned on other proteins is therefore a less precise calibrator than on TraitGym's shared feature pipeline. The gap is of the same order as TraitGym's chrom-LOO result ($0.077$), which is the appropriate reference: both involve evaluating coverage on a distribution the calibration fold did not include.
(iii) \emph{Large gap diagnostic of distribution shift.} The $0.04$--$0.10$ range is diagnostic of bounded shift, not of coverage collapse: marginal coverage remains at $0.89$ on aggregate, and the six outlier assays that drop below $0.85$ have AUPRC ranging from $0.24$ (CD19, base-predictor failure) to $0.97$ (OXDA\_RHOTO\_activity, base predictor strong but conformal calibration mistransfers). HCCP degrades gracefully.

\paragraph{Diagnostic: how does HCCP fail on OOD outliers?}
We probed the abstention behaviour of HCCP on the 6 cov$<0.85$ outlier assays vs.\ the 44 inliers. Three findings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Base-predictor quality predicts coverage but not abstention: Spearman$(\mathrm{AUPRC}, \mathrm{cov}) = 0.46$ ($p = 0.001$), but Spearman$(\mathrm{AUPRC}, \mathrm{singleton\ frac}) = -0.007$ ($p = 0.96$).
    \item Outlier and inlier singleton fractions are statistically indistinguishable: $0.378 \pm 0.014$ (outlier) vs.\ $0.355 \pm 0.026$ (inlier), $\Delta = 0.023$ (within one inlier std).
    \item Empty prediction sets (the most conservative output) are produced for $0/50$ assays.
\end{itemize}
Together these say HCCP's coverage degradation on novel proteins is \emph{not} mediated by adaptive abstention. Coverage failures are concentrated in \emph{confident-but-wrong singletons}: on a new protein, $\shat$ trained on 216 source assays underestimates aleatoric uncertainty in regions where the base predictor lacks coverage, so the conformal threshold remains permissive when it should not. This is an honest limitation that points to a clear future-work direction --- per-assay $\shat$ adaptation, e.g., via a small held-out fragment of target-protein labels (analogous to the calibration-set fine-tuning of \citet{tibshirani2019weighted} for covariate shift). It does \emph{not} invalidate the bin-conditional T3 guarantee, which holds within the calibration distribution; it characterizes where T3's domain assumption (A1 chrom-group exchangeability, transferred here to assay-group exchangeability) becomes load-bearing.

\paragraph{Why this matters for the main paper.} ProteinGym differs from TraitGym along three orthogonal axes: benchmark domain (protein fitness vs.\ variant pathogenicity), feature pipeline (physicochemical vs.\ pre-computed genomic foundation models), and label taxonomy (DMS fitness bins vs.\ ClinVar / GWAS). HCCP's coverage degrades gracefully on both benchmarks --- marginal coverage within one finite-sample slack of target on aggregate, with locally bounded $\shat$-bin gap distribution (median $0.042$ on ProteinGym, $0.077$ on TraitGym chrom-LOO) --- consistent with the theoretical guarantees (T1 marginal, T2 class-cond, T3 $\shat$-bin-local) being properties of the conformal calibration rather than artifacts of TraitGym alone.

\subsection{Feature engineering details}
\label{app:proteingym:features}

The 67-dimensional feature vector concatenates: (a) one-hot encodings of wild-type AA ($20$) and mutant AA ($20$); (b) position normalized by sequence length ($1$); (c) BLOSUM62 score for the WT$\to$MT substitution ($1$); (d) Kyte-Doolittle hydrophobicity delta ($1$); (e) van der Waals volume delta ($1$, Chothia 1975 values); (f) charge delta at pH 7 ($1$); (g) Grantham polarity delta ($1$); (h) a mismatch flag indicating whether the reported WT AA matches the target sequence at the given position ($1$); (i) local amino-acid composition in the $\pm 5$ AA window around the mutation site ($20$). Total: $20 + 20 + 7 + 20 = 67$. Multi-mutants (e.g., double substitutions \texttt{A1C:D2E}) are excluded, retaining $\approx 78\%$ of the raw rows per assay. Extractor code: \texttt{scripts/37\_proteingym\_features.py}.
