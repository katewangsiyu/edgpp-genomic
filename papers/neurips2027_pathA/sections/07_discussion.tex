\paragraph{Clinical implications.}
HCCP prediction sets are \emph{not} a replacement for expert review; they are a calibrated ``second opinion'' that delineates which variants a VEP model can confidently classify. A singleton set $\pset(x) = \{k\}$ is a confident call; a set containing both labels or neither is an explicit abstention signal. On TraitGym Mendelian, HCCP produces singletons for $\approx$80\% of variants at $\alpha = 0.10$; the remaining 20\% are flagged for expert review, with coverage maintained by construction. This framing complements selective prediction \citep{elyaniv2010selective}: whereas selective prediction maximizes accuracy at a chosen coverage, conformal prediction guarantees coverage at any chosen error rate.

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
\ref{ass:A1} (chrom-group exchangeability) is load-bearing. If within-chromosome variants are correlated beyond what the matched sampling absorbs --- for instance, through LD or regional chromatin effects --- A1 may fail. Empirically, the chrom-LOO protocol of TraitGym \citep{benegas2025traitgym} mitigates this by treating each chromosome as an independent fold; our within-cell KS audit (12.3\% rejection) suggests mild but manageable violation. T3$'$ absorbs the residual gap via the TV term. \ref{ass:ASL} (score-Lipschitz) is required only for the T5 adaptive $K$ derivation; T3 is valid without it. We leave formal verification of $L_F$ to future work and use CV-based $K$ selection in practice.

\paragraph{Mendelian--Complex asymmetry.}
The cross-dataset experiments reveal a striking directional asymmetry: C$\to$M transfer preserves coverage ($0.901$), while M$\to$C collapses ($0.738$). This is consistent with the biological intuition that complex-trait variants occupy a broader feature space (polygenic, small effects) that subsumes the monogenic signal, but not vice versa. The Barber T4 proxy bound correctly predicts the collapse direction (§\ref{sec:experiments}). From a deployment perspective, a model calibrated on complex traits may generalize to rare-disease variants, but not the reverse.

\paragraph{Epistemic vs.\ aleatoric uncertainty.}
Our DEGU-lite comparison (§\ref{sec:experiments}) shows that ensemble disagreement ($\shat_{\mathrm{DEGU}}$) and supervised residual regression ($\shat_{\mathrm{HCCP}}$) capture different aspects of uncertainty. On Mendelian (strong signal, well-separated classes), ensemble $\shat$ achieves comparable local coverage. On Complex (weak signal, dominant aleatoric noise), supervised $\shat$ provides $4.3\times$ better worst-cell coverage. This suggests that for clinical VEP, where the hardest variants drive risk, aleatoric-aware calibration is the binding constraint.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(i) Our cross-dataset TV proxy underestimates the true swap-TV by $\approx$0.036 (honest gap flagged in §\ref{sec:experiments}); closing this requires access to an oracle model.
(ii) The T5 rate $O(n^{-1/2})$ assumes equiprobable bins; non-uniform partitions could improve constants but not the rate.
(iii) TraitGym's matched sampling design controls for consequence type, MAF, and TSS distance, which suppresses distributional heterogeneity that would be present in a clinical cohort.
(iv) The oracle bin count $K^\star$ derived in Theorem~\ref{thm:t5oracle} is a \emph{large-sample upper bound}; in the finite-sample regime ($n \leq 11{,}400$ on TraitGym) the binding constraint is the per-cell floor $n_{k,b} \geq \lceil 1/\alpha \rceil$, and CV-selected $\hat K_{\mathrm{CV}}$ is substantially smaller than $K^\star$ (App.~\ref{app:tables:ksweep}). We recommend using $\hat K_{\mathrm{CV}}$ in practice and treating $K^\star$ as asymptotic guidance.
(v) We did not evaluate AlphaGenome \citep{avsec2026alphagenome} as a feature backbone due to GPU budget constraints; the HCCP framework is backbone-agnostic and the extension is mechanical.
(vi) We compare against a GBM-backbone reimplementation of DEGU (DEGU-lite, §\ref{sec:experiments}) to isolate the conformal contribution from the backbone choice; a full TF/Keras CNN reimplementation of DEGU \citep{zhou2026degu} on TraitGym is deferred to future work.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
\textbf{Clinical deployment risks.} VEP systems inform consequential decisions (cascade testing, reproductive choices, preventive surgery). Miscalibrated confidence can silently push users toward overconfident rare-disease diagnoses or dismissive classification of ambiguous variants. HCCP provides \emph{calibrated} abstention, but abstention does not \emph{guarantee} clinical safety: the remaining $\approx 20\%$ singletons on Mendelian still carry a $10\%$ finite-sample miscoverage floor, and the $\{0, 1\}$ abstention signal must be paired with human expert review policy to translate coverage into clinical utility.
\textbf{Population representativeness.} TraitGym is built from ClinVar (Mendelian) and GWAS catalog (Complex), both of which over-represent individuals of European ancestry. Coverage guarantees derived on TraitGym do not automatically transfer to under-represented populations; operationalizing HCCP in a diverse clinical setting requires re-calibration on population-stratified folds, which is a direct application of the Mondrian machinery (partition by ancestry group) but requires data we do not have.
\textbf{Downstream misuse.} A singleton prediction $\pset(x) = \{1\}$ is a \emph{necessary but not sufficient} condition for pathogenicity: ACMG/AMP \citep{richards2015acmg} consensus explicitly treats computational predictions as evidence category PP3/BP4 (supporting), not stand-alone classification. Positioning HCCP singletons as diagnostic is incorrect use; positioning them as one calibrated evidence line in a multi-evidence framework is the intended use. We encourage downstream integrators to document which prediction sets feed which ACMG evidence tier and to track population-stratified coverage post-deployment.
